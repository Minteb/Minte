Background

My Story

Growing up in Africa, I saw firsthand how uncertain harvests ripple into uncertain futures. That observation became a question: can the view from space help us understand and protect the landscapes that feed us? Pursuing that question led me into geospatial science — a field that sits at the intersection of technology, environment, and human need.

Today, as a PhD researcher, I work with multi-temporal satellite imagery, machine learning models, and cloud-based Earth observation platforms to map cropland, estimate yields, and detect the fingerprints of climate change on agricultural landscapes. My tools span ArcGIS and Google Earth Engine to Python-based deep learning pipelines.

But I am equally motivated by the work that happens after the analysis. Long-term, I am committed to knowledge transfer and capacity building across Africa — training the next generation of spatial scientists who can translate satellite data into decisions at the local level, where food security is won or lost.

Publications

Satellite-Based Crop Type Mapping in Smallholder Systems: A Machine Learning Approach Using Sentinel-2 Time Series

Minte et al. · Remote Sensing of Environment · 2024

A Random Forest classification framework applied to dense multi-temporal Sentinel-2 stacks to map seven dominant crop types across heterogeneous smallholder landscapes in Ethiopia, achieving an overall accuracy of 87.4%.

→ View Paper

Assessing Climate Change Impacts on Crop Yield Using Earth Observation and Statistical Downscaling in the Ethiopian Highlands

Minte et al. · Agricultural and Forest Meteorology · 2023

Integration of ERA5 reanalysis climate data with MODIS-derived NDVI phenology to model historical yield variability and project future productivity under RCP4.5 and RCP8.5 scenarios.

→ View Paper

Land Use / Land Cover Dynamics in the Kebena Watershed: A Multi-Decadal Remote Sensing Analysis

Minte, co-authors · Land Degradation & Development · 2022

Quantified LULC transitions from 1990–2020 in the Kebena watershed using Landsat time series, revealing a 34% decline in natural vegetation cover and its hydrological implications for downstream communities.
